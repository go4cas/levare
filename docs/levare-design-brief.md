# levare — UI and identity design brief

**For:** Claude Design
**From:** Cas (the conductor)
**Companions:** three working prototypes — `conductor-studio-view.html`, `conductor-project-view.html`, `conductor-run-view.html` — plus `levare-brand.html` (a concept sheet, superseded in part by this brief). The prototypes define information architecture and interaction, not final visuals. You are being briefed to give this product its real visual identity; the prototypes are the skeleton you must not break.

---

## What this product is

levare (pronounced leh-VAH-reh — Italian, the conductor's preparatory lift before the downbeat) is a solo operator's console for an AI agent workforce. One human (the **Conductor** — always the user, never software) directs a studio of agent teams that take products from pitch to production. An LLM agent called the **Orchestrator** interprets the Conductor's intent and narrates the system; a deterministic process called the **Runner** executes declared flows; agent **members** do the work. All state lives as markdown files with YAML frontmatter in a git repo — the UI is a stateless projection of that repo, re-derived on every request. It should feel like a precision instrument built for one person, not a SaaS dashboard built for a market.

The name encodes the product's core invariant: nothing runs until the Conductor gives the beat. Tagline: **"Agents play on your beat."** README long form: *levare (leh-VAH-reh) — the conductor's lift before the downbeat. Nothing runs until you give the beat.*

Audience: the Conductor is a developer and product manager with strong taste — reference points are Linear and Vercel for discipline and restraint, though the identity must not imitate either. Density is welcome; decoration is not. The register throughout is calm, factual, slightly dry. The system never celebrates, never apologizes, never persuades.

## Vocabulary (use these words exactly)

The product is **levare**, always lowercase. The **Conductor** is the human. The **Orchestrator** is the chat agent (panel title: "Orchestrator"; it speaks in first person, briefing-style). The **Runner** is the deterministic process (appears only as a timeline actor; machinery, never personified). A **work unit** is one run of the pipeline (types: inception, feature, fix, spike, research). An **artifact** is a produced markdown file with status and lineage. A **gate** is a decision waiting on the Conductor. A **team** is a declared flow of **members** (agents). The **score** is the run view's step rail. The **registry** is the browsable set of definitions (teams, agents, skills, knowledge, types, connectors, evals). Copy referring to the user says "you"; gates are "on you" or "awaiting you."

## Identity

**Logo — yours to design; here is the concept vocabulary.** The raw material: the baton; the *ictus* (the precise instant the beat lands under the baton); the levare gesture itself, the lift before the downbeat; and kinship with the score rail's language of circles (steps) and diamonds (gates). An earlier concept drew the wordmark's *l* as a baton stroke with an accent dot at its base as the ictus — it demos well but risks reading as "evare," which is exactly the failure to avoid. Hard constraints: the wordmark must read "levare" unambiguously to a cold reader — any letterform play is optional garnish, never load-bearing; the mark must survive 16px (favicon, terminal glyph); it must work in pure monochrome; no container shapes, no gradients. Whether the ictus-dot idea survives is your call.

**Color.** **Ink** `#191B21` and **Paper** `#F5F6F7` are fixed — the neutrals, swapping roles across light and dark modes. The **accent is open**, with one hard ban: no violet/indigo family — it is the default AI-product accent and instantly reads as generated. The accent appears sparingly in-product (only as the Orchestrator's voice; color otherwise belongs to teams), so it can afford to be bolder and stranger than a typical UI primary. Three starting directions, in order of the Conductor's current preference: **Podium** — opera-house vermilion, around `#C2402A`, the conductor's red; **Verdigris** — aged-brass patina around `#1F6E5E` (caution: adjacent to a sibling product's teal, and teal-plus-amber is its own cliché); **Ultramarine ink** around `#2743C0` (caution: blue-accent-on-gray is the Linear silhouette). **Gate color** stays in the amber/brass attention family — its scarcity is the product's personality — but must shed the default soft-amber-pill look; direction: a drier, inkier brass around `#8A6414`, more marking-pencil than warning-chip. Gates must never become red: red means error, and a gate is an invitation. Whatever you choose, deliver light- and dark-mode values plus the derivation rules.

**Type.** Choose the mono first: it carries semantic weight in-product (see below) and defines half the texture. Preferred: **Berkeley Mono** (licensed) — the quiet signature of tools-for-craftspeople; free alternatives in order: **Commit Mono**, **IBM Plex Mono**. JetBrains Mono is acceptable but is the default of dev-tool defaults. For the UI sans: **Switzer** (Fontshare) or **Hanken Grotesk** — banned: Inter (a third of the generated look on its own), Söhne (another AI lab's face), Geist (Vercel's). One idea worth exploring: since levare is an Italian musical term, the wordmark alone may take a serif or italic moment (e.g. Fraunces italic) — a single flash of musicality at the identity level, with zero of it inside the instrument, which stays strictly grotesque plus mono.

## The semantic system (hard constraints — do not reassign these channels)

**Color means team — on identity surfaces only.** Every team declares one hue in its definition file. Team hue appears wherever an *actor or entity is identified* — avatars, registry swatches, flow strips, the avatar column on score steps — and never conveys status. Members wear the color of the team whose flow they are running in. The Orchestrator wears the brand accent — the identity and the agent speak in one voice. The renderer owns derivation rules: tint generation via color-mix, a contrast floor for avatar text (white on a raw light hue must be corrected automatically), and a minimum perceptual distance from the Podium accent and gate brass so user-declared hues cannot impersonate system colors.

**Gate color means "needs you."** Gate brass is a member of the state palette below — the needs-you state. It appears exclusively on gates: gate cards, gate diamonds, pending badges. Never place it on a non-gate element; a step awaiting the Conductor is represented by its own brass diamond node, not a brass-tinted circle. Gates must never become red: red means failure, and a gate is an invitation. Its scarcity is the product working. *(Amended by the message-severity scale below: gate brass is no longer the only amber-family value in the product, but within its own channel — entity lifecycle state — it remains the only one, unchanged.)*

**Status is the canonical state palette** (conductor amendment, supersedes the earlier fill-state rule). One system-owned palette expresses lifecycle state for *every* stateful entity — work units, artifacts, score nodes, mini-scores, chips: **done** = solid green; **active** = blue with pulse; **waiting** = hollow neutral; **blocked** = hollow neutral plus an explicit label (never orange — orange near brass is forbidden); **needs-you** = gate brass, gates only; **failed** = red. There is no general-purpose "warn" hue *on the status palette*: an entity's lifecycle state that is tempted toward amber is either needs-you (brass, gate-shaped) or failed (red), or it renders neutral with text — status chips, dots, and score nodes never reach for the message-severity amber defined below; that channel is for *messages*, not *entities*, and the two must never be visually interchangeable. Shape stays a separate channel: circles are steps, diamonds are gates, dashed marks the start gate. State is system semantics and never varies by team.

**Message severity is a second, independent scale — NOTE / WARNING / DANGER** (conductor amendment, NOTES UI12, closing the gap NOTES UI11 documented). The status palette above answers "what lifecycle state is this *entity* in?" Nothing previously answered "how seriously should the Conductor take this *message*?" — a callout, an inline caveat, a scoping note. NOTES UI11 hit this gap directly: the C13 subscription-scoping note on a connector card is genuinely a warning, but the brief's blanket amber ban left it "structure without colour" (a tinted panel with no hue, wearing the same neutral ink as an ordinary note). That was a missing vocabulary, not a correct ruling — this amendment supplies it without touching the status palette's own amber rule above, which stands exactly as written for entity lifecycle.

Three levels, ordered by how much attention they demand:

- **NOTE** — neutral ink. A quiet info affordance for contextual information the Conductor may want but doesn't need to act on. Same tinted-panel shape as the other two severities, but the tint, border, and icon all stay in the neutral ink scale (no hue at all) — visually the quietest of the three, and the one that costs the Conductor nothing to skim past.
- **WARNING** — a muted amber. "True, and you should know." A tinted panel with an amber-toned border and icon; body text stays ink (never amber) so the panel signals the severity and the prose stays maximally legible. This is the *one and only* place general-purpose amber is now permitted in the product.
- **DANGER** — the existing danger red, unchanged. Failures and blocked reasons. It was always implicitly the top of a severity scale; this amendment just makes that formal. Danger red is deliberately the *same* red as the status palette's `failed` state — both encode one meaning, "this is bad," and reusing the hue across the two channels is consistent with, not a violation of, the one-hue-one-meaning rule (unlike amber, where gate brass and message-warning are two genuinely different meanings that must not share a hue).

**The amber rule, refined, not repealed.** Amber is reserved exclusively for *warning severity* on messages, exactly as gate brass is reserved exclusively for *needs-you* on entities — one hue, one meaning, per channel. The two ambers must never be the same value: a warning callout must not be mistakable for a gate at a glance, so message-warning amber is a distinct, more muted swatch than gate brass's drier marking-pencil brass. Neither amber ever appears outside its own channel — gate brass never tints a message, warning amber never tints a status chip, dot, score node, or gate element.

**Token values.** Light mode: warning `#96591C` (gate brass, unchanged, stays `#8A6414`). Dark mode: warning `#E2A25A` (gate brass, unchanged, stays `#C99A3C`). Panel wash and border derive the same way the gate treatment already does — `color-mix(in oklab, var(--warning) N%, var(--panel)/var(--border-strong))` — never a hand-picked literal, so both themes stay correct by construction. NOTE and DANGER need no new tokens: NOTE reads the existing `--fg`/`--border` neutrals, DANGER reads the existing `--danger` token the status palette already defines.

**Shape glyph means work unit type.** A small monochrome glyph from the type's definition file appears in exactly three places: project view unit rows, the gate inbox, and the run view header. Nowhere else. Current set: inception `◈`, feature `▸`, fix `◦`; **spike** and **research** need glyphs from you — spike should read as ephemeral/disposable (its code never ships; its output is findings), research as document-ish (its terminal artifact is a report). Type also implies weight: spike and research units have short scores and fewer gates, so their rows read lighter by structure alone — the glyph confirms, never carries.

**Mono typeface means filesystem truth.** Artifact ids, paths, branches, timestamps, repo names render in the mono face — and every mono token is a link (internal entities route to rendered views; repos, branches, releases, and deploy URLs route out). Prose, judgment, and narration render in the UI face. This split is doing real semantic work; keep it legible.

## The interaction constitution (hard constraints)

**Read everywhere, act in one place.** The only actionable element in the entire product is the gate card, plus the Orchestrator composer. No edit affordances on scores, timelines, or project cards; no "new work unit" buttons; no settings screens. Creation and configuration happen by talking to the Orchestrator or editing files.

**One gate card, every level.** The gate card has a fixed anatomy — kind marker, artifact name, producer, one-line context, lineage line, age, and verbs — and appears identically in the studio inbox, the project view, and the run view chat. Verbs are approve, request changes, and reject (start gates: start, not yet, re-scope). Request changes opens an inline note field. Resolved cards collapse to a quiet single-line record and persist in place; the chat transcript is the decision log's human face.

**The Orchestrator panel is the single entry point.** It is present on every screen at the same scope as the screen. It opens with a briefing, not a greeting. Actions elsewhere in the UI (review gate, open run) route through it rather than opening modals. There are no modals.

**The UI is a projection.** Every screen states its derivation quietly (the prototypes use a subtitle like "derived from work/ on every request"); provenance lines like "last commit 2d ago by cas" appear where definitions render. The registry's only write is "Edit source" — raw markdown with a validity check and "Save and commit." No form-based authoring anywhere, ever.

## Screens

**Studio landing** — the Conductor's inbox. Order is load-bearing: stats strip (gates on you, members running, units shipped 30d, median gate response — the last is deliberately public; the Conductor is the system's scheduler and their latency is its health), then Needs You (the cross-project gate inbox, decidable without click-through), then Running Now (live member processes with team color and elapsed time), then project cards last. Left rail: team registry with declared colors, connector health from doctor, recent releases, and a quiet **ideas** list — captured pitches with no project yet (from `ideas/`), deliberately the most understated element on the page: a backlog, not a to-do; no counts, no urgency styling, never gate-colored. Right rail: Orchestrator at studio scope.

**Project view** — one product's state. Left: the project pointer rendered (repo, deploy, pace, house rules) and the constitution — founding artifacts with computed citation counts and visible supersede history — plus releases. Center: work unit rows, each with type glyph, status chip, one-sentence summary, and a mini-score (the score's dot-strip compression; its length telegraphs unit type). Rows expand in place to an artifact list with lineage and two actions: open run view, review gate (which summons the card into the Orchestrator panel). Right: Orchestrator at project scope.

**Run view** — one work unit live. Three columns: the score (vertical rail — circles, diamonds, the signature element of the product; state nodes in the canonical palette plus a small team-tinted avatar per step, so a viewer reads run state and who has the ball as two adjacent columns, from across the room), the timeline (every runner walk, member spawn, and gate event; actor avatars — agent initials on team tint, the Conductor as the only solid-filled disc, the Runner deliberately gray; a blinking marker on the live process), and the Orchestrator, where gates arrive as cards with narrated context before them.

**Registry** — the definition browser (earlier prototype round; anatomy stands). Sidebar of entity types; rendered view per entity (teams show their flow strip; agents show their context recipe as navigable pills; skills render SKILL.md with bundled scripts listed and vendored provenance visible; knowledge shows injected-into backlinks; types show expected kinds; evals show golden inputs and last results). "Edit source" toggles raw markdown.

Every entity name anywhere in the product links into the registry; every unit path links into the run view. The product should feel like one continuous, fully cross-linked surface over a single repo.

## Aesthetic direction

The result should be modern and slick — and it must not look AI-generated. No template tells: no gradient-mesh heroes, no generic dashboard chrome, no violet-on-white SaaS styling, no default-stack choices that could belong to any product. Every choice should feel made for this instrument specifically.

The prototypes' direction — light, cool-neutral, hairline borders, generous quiet — is a starting point the Conductor likes, not a mandate. You own type (within the slate above), spacing, exact accent values (team hues are user data and will vary; design the derivation rules, not the hues), motion, and both modes: light and dark are equal first-class citizens, and the semantic system must survive both — the canonical state palette and gate-color scarcity in particular. Take one real aesthetic risk; the score rail is the natural place to spend it — it is the product's signature and currently its plainest realization.

Motion: micro-animations and micro-interactions are wanted throughout — hover and focus states, press feedback, card expand/collapse, gate resolution, count transitions — but always fast, physical, and quiet; polish that is felt more than noticed. The pulse on live things and gate arrival remain the only *attention-seeking* animations; everything else supports, and reduced-motion must be respected.

Known issues to solve properly rather than inherit: low-saturation team hues make tinted avatar discs illegible (design a contrast floor into the derivation rules); the start-gate dashed border is a placeholder; mobile is an untested afterthought in the prototypes and deserves a real answer, likely inbox-and-Orchestrator first.

## Deliverables

The identity: logo (mark plus wordmark, both modes, down to 16px), final accent and gate colors with light/dark values and derivation rules, and the type pairing licensed or free as chosen. Then high-fidelity passes of the four screens (desktop; mobile for studio landing at minimum), and the component vocabulary as a small system — gate card in all states, score and mini-score, artifact row, actor avatar with derivation rules, status chips, type glyphs, Orchestrator panel and message anatomy, pointer and stat cards — stated so every rule can be implemented as a pure function of frontmatter values. Hard technical constraint: no front-end frameworks — no React, Vue, Svelte, or similar. Everything must be realizable in vanilla HTML/CSS/JS served by a single Bun binary, so design within what modern CSS and small hand-written scripts can deliver (which is a lot — lean on it).
